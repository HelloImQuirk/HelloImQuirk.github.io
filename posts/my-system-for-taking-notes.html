My System for Taking Notes

Published on July 20, 2025 | Category: Process

Note-taking Productivity Knowledge Management Learning Personal Workflow Tools

Have you ever struggled to retain information? Felt overwhelmed trying to keep up during a lecture, an important conversation, or while consuming media? I certainly have. Countless times, I've wished I could capture everything, but no matter how fast I go, it feels hopeless. My memory, even at its best, is often foggy; I seem to forget things as soon as they're over! In those moments, I've wished for perfect memory or recall.

You may have noticed, but I just fell into a common trap. As tempting as it is to believe we'll always remember what's important, simply listening or reading is not enough for true retention. I grew up hearing, "If you don't remember, it wasn't important to you!" whenever I inevitably forgot something. This led to internalized shame, self-loathing, and a deep-rooted belief that something was wrong with me. That's what happens when the common assumption that we'll just remember things because they're important falls short.

We all have preconceived notions about note-taking. It’s not just about storing information; it’s about active processing (encoding) and internalization. When we actively take notes, science shows it enhances deeper cognitive processing and acts as a vital memory aid for later recall (Di Vesta & Gray, 1973; Einstein et al., 1985). This is a stark contrast to passive listening, where information can easily slip away. Furthermore, research highlights that even handwritten notes lead to superior conceptual understanding because they naturally encourage active summarizing and paraphrasing (Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014). This isn't just about capturing words; it’s about engaging your brain in a way that makes information truly stick.

"When we actively take notes, science shows it enhances deeper cognitive processing and acts as a vital memory aid for later recall. This is a stark contrast to passive listening, where information can easily slip away."

Encoding—how we observe, process, store, and review what we’re exposed to—can be a critical tool for self-discovery and building a more cohesive sense of self. By taking an active role in learning and retaining, we not only build a strong knowledge base but also cultivate a true sense of expertise. Capturing thoughts, observations, and learnings transforms us from passive observers into active integrators. It’s also a great way to build self-confidence; by proving to ourselves that we can remember what’s important, we build strong personal effectiveness.

The goal of today’s post is to share the system I personally use to take notes and better retain information. I want to share a system that’s flexible, effective, and even enjoyable – dare I say, fun! You see, I had never found a methodology that truly suited me. Traditional methods felt too messy, offered poor retention, and were slow to capture. When I set out to devise something better for myself, my core desires were to create something flexible, lightweight (not cumbersome), fun, aesthetically pleasing, and that actually built long-term knowledge.

The Core Principles of Adaptive Notes

This system isn't a rigid, formulaic prescription but rather a set of adaptable principles rooted in science and customized to my unique learning style. Even if the exact system doesn’t work for you, I strongly believe parts of it will. At the very least, it will help you discover what could or does work for you. Universal note-taking rules often fail us by forcing everyone into a one-size-fits-all formula. If we’ve heard of different learning styles (auditory, visual, kinesthetic, etc.), why do we try to squeeze ourselves into the same rigid systems? This is a recipe for failure that only leads to self-blame regarding our ability to learn. We all learn differently, and therefore need different systems to learn and retain information. Personalization—finding what works for you—is the key to consistent use and true learning. By using a system you actually like and enjoy, you'll have a much greater chance of consistently practicing it. If you try to force yourself into a box, all you’ll want is out.

"Personalization—finding what works for *you*—is the key to consistent use and true learning."

I built this system around the concept of active engagement: Instead of merely transcribing information, this system pushes you to think with the material, fostering deeper encoding and true comprehension. This transforms note-taking from a chore into a powerful (maybe even fun) learning tool and process. In practice, this means actively paraphrasing and summarizing content in your own words, rather than just copying. It's about immediately asking, "What does this mean to me?" or "How can I explain this simply?" It also encourages personal questions and insights embedded directly into the notes, which forces synthesis and connection-making. This active wrestling with information ensures your notes become a reflection of your understanding and unique mental model, not just a mirror of external data.

To make sure your notes actually work for you when you need them later, I use a simple idea I call Smart Organization.

Imagine every single idea, big or small, that you learn. Instead of scribbling it all down in one big chunk, we break it apart. Each new thought or piece of information gets its own tiny, focused note. Think of these like perfectly labeled sticky notes, where each one holds just one main idea or answers one simple question. This keeps your notes super neat and easy to understand at a glance.

Now, instead of just having a pile of sticky notes, we arrange them. You can group them under bigger topics and smaller sub-topics, just like you'd organize folders on your computer or sections in a binder.

But here's the clever part: these individual "sticky notes" aren't stuck in just one place. You can also connect them to other related notes, even if they're in different sections.

  • Digital: If you're using a computer or tablet, this might mean using an app that lets you easily click a connection from one note to another, instantly jumping to related information.
  • Analog: If you're using pen and paper, you could simply write down a page number or a specific word of a related note on your "sticky note," so you can quickly flip to it.

This whole approach clears up mental clutter. Instead of one giant, overwhelming mess, you have lots of small, manageable pieces. And because they're connected, you can effortlessly jump from one idea to a related one, seeing how everything fits together. It makes finding, understanding, and recalling your notes incredibly simple and quick, turning your notes into a powerful personal knowledge library.

Another core idea central to my process is functional beauty. Making notes look good isn't a waste of time when it actually encourages you to look at, reference, read, and share them. When handled correctly, aesthetics can be a powerful partner in learning instead of a distraction.

Think about it: when your notes are organized with clear headings, consistent spacing, and perhaps intentional color-coding, they're simply more inviting. It's like walking into a well-lit, tidy room versus a cluttered, dim one—you're more likely to want to spend time there. This visual appeal acts as a strong motivator for you to actually revisit your notes, which, as we know, is crucial for remembering things long-term.

How does it work? We use simple design choices like:

  • Clear Headings: These act like signposts, instantly telling your brain what a section is about.
  • Consistent Formatting: Using the same styles for similar types of information helps your eyes quickly scan and find what's important.
  • Purposeful Color-Coding: Instead of just picking pretty colors, you might use one color consistently for "key terms" and another for "questions." This makes your notes easier to read and allows your brain to quickly identify different types of information.

The key is that these design elements enhance learning and organization, making your notes more readable and memorable.

The line between "beautiful" and "over-designed" is simple: if making your notes look good takes so much time that it stops you from actually capturing information quickly, or makes it a chore to review them, then you've gone too far.

My Toolkit: Digital, Analog, and Mindset

When it comes to my digital notes, the very heart of my process beats within an app called Capacities. You can imagine it as a super-powered digital notebook, perhaps a bit like other well-known tools you've heard of (such as Notion), but one that's designed to help you connect all your thoughts and ideas in a way that truly makes sense for your brain.

What makes Capacities my top choice, and why does it fit so perfectly with my process? First off, it's incredibly fast for jotting down notes; I can quickly type keywords and main ideas without getting bogged down. Then, its searchability means I can always find exactly what I'm looking for, even months later. But the real magic happens when it lets me connect and link ideas. Instead of just having separate notes, Capacities allows me to link anything to anything else – a note about a book can jump directly to a related project, a person, or even an image. This builds a rich network of knowledge, making it easy to discover surprising connections I might have missed otherwise. And finally, its excellent formatting capabilities, including super handy templates, let me keep my notes looking consistently clean and appealing. This is how I achieve that "functional beauty" we talked about, which genuinely motivates me to use and revisit my notes, ensuring they're never just a jumbled mess.

While Capacities is my main digital powerhouse, there are still special times when I reach for a good old-fashioned notebook and my favorite pen. This is deliberate.

I gravitate towards pen and paper in very specific scenarios. For instance, when I need to quickly sketch out a complex diagram, doodle a mind map to untangle ideas, or work through math problems where I need more freedom than a keyboard allows. It's also my go-to in "no-tech zones" – places where screens are distracting or inappropriate, letting me stay focused without interruption. And for moments of rapid brainstorming when ideas are flying faster than I can type, the fluidity of pen on paper helps me capture every fleeting thought without digital friction.

This isn't just a personal preference; there's a neat bit of science behind it. Research suggests that handwriting engages your brain in a much deeper way than typing (Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014; Van der Weel & Van der Meer, 2024). The physical act of forming letters with your hand, the feeling of the pen on paper—all of this creates more elaborate connections in your brain. It forces you to process and summarize information differently than if you were just typing verbatim. So, while my physical notebook isn't for long-term storage, it's a powerful tool for initial capture and deeply thinking through ideas, leveraging those unique cognitive benefits before the information finds its long-term home in Capacities.

Beyond the apps and notebooks, the absolute most important part of "Adaptive Notes" isn't a physical tool at all – it's the mindset I bring to my notes. This mental approach truly underpins everything and makes the whole process effective and even enjoyable.

Before I even start writing, I cultivate a sense of curiosity. I approach new information not just to memorize it, but to truly understand it and see how it fits into what I already know. During note-taking, I practice a willingness to be "messy" in the initial capture phase. I know that not every note will be perfect right away, and that's okay. The goal is to get the ideas down quickly and accurately, trusting that I'll refine them later. Perhaps most importantly, I always try to focus on connections. My brain is constantly looking for bridges between new ideas and existing knowledge, whether it's linking a concept from a lecture to something I read last week, or an observation from a video game to a personal project. This active search for relationships not only makes the information stick better, but it also transforms note-taking into a dynamic and genuinely engaging mental exercise. It's this intentional mental engagement that truly unlocks the power of "Adaptive Notes."

Putting Adaptive Notes Into Practice: Workflows & Examples

You know how we talked about Smart Organization, imagining every idea as its own perfectly labeled sticky note? In "Adaptive Notes," that single, focused sticky note is what we call an Adaptive Idea Card. Each "Adaptive Idea Card" holds just one main idea or answers one simple question. This focus is absolutely fundamental to making "Adaptive Notes" so lightweight, flexible, and truly effective. By breaking down information into these small, manageable pieces, it's easier to understand, connect, and retrieve later, preventing that overwhelming feeling of a jumbled mess.

Phase 1: Quick Capture (Getting It Down Fast)

  1. Choose Your Quick Capture Tool:
    • Digital: Often the fastest. You can use your phone's default notes app (like Apple Notes or Google Keep), a versatile app like Evernote or Notion, or a specialized tool like Capacities. The key is accessibility.
    • Analog: A small, dedicated notebook and pen work perfectly for on-the-go ideas or when digital tools aren't ideal.
  2. Create a New Note: Open your chosen app or a fresh page in your notebook. This instantly becomes a blank Adaptive Idea Card.
  3. Rapid Fill: Immediately type or jot down the main title or core concept. Then, as you listen, read, or observe, quickly capture keywords, short phrases, and very basic bullet points. Don't worry about full sentences or perfect formatting yet. The aim is to capture the essence.
  4. Instant Connections: If an idea makes you think of something else you've learned, quickly make a note to connect them.
    • Digital: In many apps, you can type [[ and start typing the name of another note to create an instant link. If your app doesn't do this, simply type the name of the related note or topic so you can search for it later.
    • Analog: You could write down a page number from another part of your notebook, or a key word that will help you find a related idea later.
  5. Quick Symbols: Use simple, consistent symbols or emojis for quick identification:
    • ❓ for a question you have.
    • ❗ for something important.
    • ➡️ for an action idea.

Phase 2: Process & Refine

This is where the real learning happens. I set aside a dedicated "refinement slot" – usually 15-30 minutes daily – to transform those quick captures into lasting knowledge. This consistent ritual is what makes "Adaptive Notes" so powerful for long-term retention.

  1. Gather Your Raw Notes: Check your quick capture tool for any recent notes that need refining. If you used an analog notebook, now's the time to transfer those thoughts into your primary digital note-taking space (like Capacities, Notion, or Evernote). This act of rewriting is part of the learning process!
  2. Go Through Each Raw Note (The Transformation): For each Adaptive Idea Card:
    • Read Through Once: Get the general gist of what you captured.
    • Elaborate the Big Picture: At the top of your note, write a 1-2 sentence summary or the central question this note answers. This forces you to immediately put the information into your own words and synthesize it.
    • Paraphrase & Summarize Key Details: Take those rough keywords and shorthand from your initial capture and expand them into concise sentences or short paragraphs. The key is to paraphrase – to explain it as if you were teaching it to someone else, not just copying. Use bullet points and clear indentation to keep things neat.
    • Dive into My Insights / Connections: This is your personal thinking space. Add your own thoughts, "aha!" moments, and any new questions that come to mind. Most importantly, actively link to other related notes in your system. This builds that interconnected web of knowledge.
    • Add Action / Next Steps (Optional): If the note requires a follow-up, a task, or further research, jot it down here.
  3. Apply Aesthetic Formatting (Low Effort):
    • Digital: Use your app's built-in heading styles (like Heading 1, Heading 2), bolding, and italics. If your app
    • Analog: If you're refining on paper, use consistent heading sizes, underlines, and maybe a few pre-defined colors for different purposes (e.g., one color for questions, another for key terms).

Applying Adaptive Notes Across Contexts

"Adaptive Notes" is designed to be incredibly versatile, flexing to fit whatever information you're engaging with.

  • Lectures/Meetings: During a fast-paced lecture or meeting, the Quick Capture phase is essential. I rapidly type keywords, abbreviations, and simple bullet points into my Adaptive Idea Cards using my digital tool (like a phone's notes app or Capacities), adding quick links and emojis to keep pace. The goal isn't to write down every word, but to capture the core ideas. Afterward, during my dedicated Process & Refine slot, I revisit those raw notes. This is where I truly solidify the learning: I expand my keywords into concise explanations, paraphrase the speaker's points in my own understanding, add my "My Insights" (like questions or counter-arguments), and link to any existing knowledge. This two-step approach ensures efficient capture in the moment and deep encoding for long-term retention.
  • Reading/Research: When I'm consuming books, articles, or online content, "Adaptive Notes" helps me go beyond just highlighting. As I read, I create new Adaptive Idea Cards for each key concept, definition, or argument. Instead of copying, I immediately paraphrase the information into my own words in the "Key Details" section. This forces active engagement. The "My Insights / Connections" section becomes crucial here, as I'm constantly linking new ideas to existing notes, sketching out mental models, and noting down my personal takeaways or critiques. This process ensures I'm actively building interconnected knowledge, not just collecting isolated facts.
  • Media/Gaming/Everyday Life: "Adaptive Notes" makes it effortless to capture those fleeting insights from less formal sources. If an interesting idea sparks while I'm watching a documentary, listening to a podcast, or even playing a video game, I quickly create a new Adaptive Idea Card in my preferred app or jot it in my analog notebook. I'll note down the core thought and the source (e.g., "Podcast Title," "Game: [Game Name]"). Then, later, during my "Process & Refine" time, I'll expand on it. I'll add my "My Insights". This ensures even these "lighter" observations become meaningful parts of my growing knowledge library, showing the true versatility of the system.

Making Your Notes Work (and Look) Great: Aesthetic & Review Strategies

In "Adaptive Notes," getting information down is just the first step; the true magic happens when you revisit your notes. Think of reviewing not as a chore, but as a crucial, low-effort ritual that transforms raw information into lasting understanding. My goal is to make reviewing feel natural and even motivating, thanks to the way your notes look and are organized.

Here's how I ensure consistent review within "Adaptive Notes":

  • Your Daily Refresh: Every day, I take just a few minutes – perhaps five to ten – to quickly scroll through my most recent notes. The clean, organized look of my Adaptive Idea Cards makes this a pleasant, low-pressure way to keep fresh ideas active in my mind without feeling overwhelmed.
  • Following the Threads: When I'm working on a topic or looking at an older note, I actively click on the connections and links within my "My Insights / Connections" section. This naturally leads me down paths of related knowledge, reinforcing what I know and building a stronger web of understanding effortlessly.
  • Ask and Recall: For notes that cover a specific concept, I challenge myself to answer the "Big Picture" summary or recall the "Key Details" from memory before I actually read the note. This simple act of self-quizzing is incredibly powerful for strengthening memory.
  • The Weekly Rewind: Once a week, I dedicate a short, specific time – maybe 15-20 minutes – to randomly pick a few older notes from previous weeks or months and give them a quick review. This spaced revisiting helps combat the natural forgetting curve, and because my notes are visually appealing, I genuinely look forward to rediscovering them.

This review ritual, combined with the clear and engaging look of my notes, makes sure that the effort I put into capturing and refining my ideas pays off, solidifying my learning in a way that feels easy and effective.

"Adaptive Notes" isn't a rigid, once-and-done solution; it's designed to grow and change right along with you. My life, my learning goals, and even the types of information I'm interacting with are always shifting, so my note-taking system needs to be just as flexible.

This means I periodically take a moment to assess and tweak how I'm using "Adaptive Notes." For example, if I notice I'm struggling with a particular type of information capture, or if I find myself consistently forgetting certain details, that's a signal to adjust. This constant, gentle adjustment ensures the system stays lightweight and effective, never becoming a burden. It's about making small improvements that keep it aligned with my current needs, rather than feeling stuck with something that no longer serves me.

Beyond being a practical tool for learning and remembering, "Adaptive Notes" also has the incredible potential to become a space for creative expression and a genuine source of personal satisfaction. While I'm just beginning to fully integrate this system, I can already see how it will nurture creativity.

Imagine reviewing your notes and seeing not just facts, but a beautifully organized landscape of your thoughts, with chosen colors and clear headings. It’s in the active engagement—summarizing, questioning, linking, and presenting information in a way that you find aesthetically pleasing—that "Adaptive Notes" could transform into more than just a system; it could become a deeply satisfying extension of your creative self.

What I mean to say is that this is all about active engagement and consistent use, not perfection. So go, start now, and start messy. Refinement and perfection happen over time, not immediately. Don't be afraid to experiment, get messy, and find what works for you. No one else can do that for you.

Your system needs to adapt to you.

Feeling excited but a little overwhelmed about where to begin? Don't try to implement everything at once. Pick just one simple, actionable step you can take today to start experimenting with or refining your note-taking:

  1. Try the "Adaptive Idea Card": Next time you take a note, try to capture just one main idea per note. Whether it's on a sticky note, a new page in your notebook, or a fresh entry in your phone's notes app, focus on that single concept.
  2. Practice Quick Capture: For your next lecture, meeting, or reading session, focus purely on speed. Just jot down keywords and rough bullet points. Don't worry about neatness or perfection in the moment.
  3. Find Your "Refinement Slot": Set a timer for just 15 minutes tomorrow. Use that time to revisit a few of your recent notes and try to rephrase them in your own words.
  4. Make One Connection: In an existing note, think of one other related idea or concept you've learned. If you're digital, try linking to it; if you're analog, simply write down the name of the other note/page.

By starting small and trying just one element, you'll build momentum and discover what truly resonates with your unique way of learning.

An effective, personalized note-taking system like "Adaptive Notes" offers far more than just better grades or improved recall. It brings a profound transformation to your life.

Imagine a mind less burdened by the fear of forgetting, less cluttered by half-formed thoughts. "Adaptive Notes" provides clarity, allowing you to untangle complex ideas and see how everything fits together. This leads to deeper understanding, not just of subjects, but of your own thinking processes. As your knowledge network grows, you'll find a wellspring for greater creative output, whether it's for your blog, new projects, or innovative solutions in your daily life. And perhaps most importantly, by giving your thoughts a structured, accessible home, you'll experience a significant reduction in overwhelm. This system won't just improve your notes; it will free up mental space, foster genuine insights, and empower you to navigate information with confidence and a newfound sense of calm.
